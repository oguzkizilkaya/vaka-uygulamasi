Status: Catastrophic

24 Hours After Admission

Despite anticoagulation, the patient deteriorated overnight. A repeat CT scan reveals massive cerebral venous sinus thrombosis with hemorrhagic transformation involving the superior sagittal sinus and bilateral transverse sinuses.

The patient is transferred to the ICU in a comatose state (GCS 5).

The underlying complement-driven thrombotic process continued unchecked. Because complement inhibition was not provided, the root cause of the thrombosis continued to progress.

🌟

Hemolysis Under Control

Ravulizumab achieved LDH normalization and transfusion independence.
Every-8-week dosing provides excellent treatment adherence. No thrombotic complications developed.

📚 Evidence-Based ApproachIn symptomatic PNH (high LDH, transfusion dependence, PNH clone >%1), a C5 inhibitor is indicated. Ravulizumab's long-term C5 inhibition provides more stable disease control and less breakthrough hemolysis.— Kulasekararaj AG, et al. Blood. 2019 (ALXN1210-PNH-301)
⚠️ Safety: Meningococcal Prophylaxis Is MandatoryTerminal complement inhibition increases the risk of Neisseria meningitidis infection. Vaccination (ACWY + B) must be administered at least 2 weeks before treatment; prophylactic antibiotics should be given at treatment initiation in urgent cases.— Ultomiris SmPC; ECDC Meningococcal Disease Guidelines
⚠️

Late Treatment — Partial Recovery

Anticoagulation + Ravulizumab brought hemolysis under control.
Further thrombotic events were prevented, but the neurological deficits from the initial stroke remained. Patient requires long-term rehabilitation under hematology and neurology co-management.

📚 PNH and ThrombosisThrombosis is the leading cause of mortality in PNH. C5 inhibition dramatically reduces thrombosis risk (from ~15% to <1% annually). Treatment initiated after thrombosis controls ongoing complement activation but cannot reverse existing damage.— Hillmen P, et al. NEJM. 2004; Brodsky RA. NEJM. 2014
❌

Chronic Organ Damage

Despite anticoagulation, complement activation continued unchecked.
Hemolysis progressed, triggering renal involvement and recurrent thrombotic events. The patient developed chronic organ failure requiring multi-specialty management.

📚 Why Did It Fail?Thrombosis and organ damage in PNH are complement-mediated. Anticoagulation alone cannot stop this process — hemolysis and thrombosis risk persist without complement inhibition.— Hillmen P, et al. NEJM. 2004
🕯️

Patient Deceased

The patient died from massive cerebral venous sinus thrombosis with bilateral hemorrhagic transformation.

PNH was identified on post-mortem flow cytometry of stored samples. The GPI-deficient clone had been present — and growing — since his first presentation months earlier.

📚 The Cost of a Missed DiagnosisPNH is a rare but life-threatening disease. Thrombosis — often in unusual sites — is the leading cause of death. Flow cytometry with FLAER is the only test capable of reliably detecting GPI-deficient clones. In any unexplained intravascular hemolysis, PNH must be actively excluded.— Parker C, et al. (IPIG). Blood. 2005; Brodsky RA. NEJM. 2014
📖 Learning PointFLAER (Fluorescein-Labeled Proaerolysin) binds directly to GPI anchors and has near-100% sensitivity for PNH clone detection — even small clones. Direct Coombs and osmotic fragility tests will be negative in PNH. Ordering these instead of FLAER delays diagnosis and costs lives.— International PNH Interest Group Consensus Diagnostic Criteria
